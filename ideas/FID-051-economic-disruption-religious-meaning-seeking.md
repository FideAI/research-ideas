# FID-051: Economic Disruption and Religious Meaning-Seeking

Status: `seed`

Track: `broader-faith-safety`

Primary need: sociology of religion, labor economics, privacy-preserving measurement, aggregate usage analysis

## Question

During periods of labor-market stress, layoffs, automation anxiety, or economic
uncertainty, do people increasingly seek religious, spiritual, existential,
vocational, or meaning-related help through AI systems?

## Why It Matters

Economic disruption can change more than employment and income. It can change
how people ask about purpose, suffering, calling, prayer, despair, family
responsibility, dignity, and hope. AI systems may become a private site where
these questions first surface, including for people who are not yet talking with
churches, families, counselors, or local institutions.

## Hypothesis

Aggregate, privacy-preserving AI usage signals may show that economic stress is
associated with increased meaning-seeking, religious inquiry, spiritual
language, prayer-like requests, vocation questions, and existential support
needs. The relationship may vary by geography, occupation exposure, age cohort,
local economic shocks, and availability of trusted community support.

## Proposed Method

- Define a taxonomy for religious, spiritual, existential, vocational, and
  meaning-seeking AI interactions without requiring access to raw conversations.
- Use privacy-preserving aggregate analysis or provider-side classification to
  measure category-level trends over time.
- Compare trends against external economic indicators such as layoff
  announcements, unemployment changes, job-exposure measures, regional shocks,
  and consumer sentiment.
- Test whether economic language and meaning-seeking categories co-occur at
  aggregate levels, such as job loss with prayer, calling, anxiety, grief, or
  purpose.
- Pair aggregate provider-side signals with consented surveys or interviews in
  churches, schools, and workforce-support settings.

## Needed Controls

- Do not request, receive, publish, or infer private conversation content.
- Treat religious identity, spiritual distress, and economic hardship as
  sensitive categories requiring strong privacy constraints.
- Avoid causal claims unless the design has credible identification.
- Distinguish seasonal religious patterns, holidays, news cycles, and ordinary
  life events from economic disruption.
- Avoid assuming that all religious or spiritual AI use is healthy, unhealthy,
  orthodox, or church-directed.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This extends `FID-046` by studying broad meaning-seeking patterns during
economic disruption, not only the experiences of workers recruited into a
field-study cohort.

## Outputs

- Privacy-preserving taxonomy for economic-disruption and meaning-seeking
  categories.
- Research design memo for aggregate provider-side analysis.
- Church-facing field brief on AI-era spiritual and vocational search.
- Measurement framework for pairing aggregate signals with local church data.
- Public claim standard for reporting sensitive aggregate trends.

## Open Questions

- Which aggregate signals can be studied without exposing private spiritual or
  economic distress?
- How should researchers distinguish explicit religion, broader spirituality,
  therapy-style language, and secular meaning-making?
- Are people seeking embodied support after AI-mediated spiritual questioning,
  or remaining in private AI-mediated reflection?
- What would churches need to know in order to respond without surveillance or
  panic?

## Ways to Help

- Contribute sociology of religion, labor economics, or privacy expertise.
- Design aggregate category taxonomies and validation methods.
- Identify external economic indicators suitable for time-series comparison.
- Recruit churches or community institutions for consented companion studies.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can study aggregate indicators of meaning-seeking during economic
disruption. It cannot identify individual users, expose private conversations,
or prove that a specific person turned to faith because of AI-related job loss.

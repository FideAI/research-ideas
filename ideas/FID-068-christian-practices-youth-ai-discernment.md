# FID-068: Christian Practices for AI-Era Youth Discernment

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: youth and young-adult ministry, practical theology, developmental research, ethics review, HAI research

## Question

Which Christian practices and community rhythms help young people examine their
AI use with agency, embodiment, truthful attention, relationship, and spiritual
discernment? What measurable outcomes and safeguards should guide future
formation-oriented research or programming?

## Why It Matters

Young people are encountering AI through school, work, social life, search, and
personal guidance. Generic digital-literacy advice may not address questions of
vocation, dependence, prayer, truthfulness, community, attention, and the
meaning of human dignity. Churches and educators need evidence before treating
any particular practice or curriculum as a solution.

## Hypothesis

Practices such as communal discernment, Sabbath and attention rhythms, service,
prayer, source reflection, and accountable peer dialogue may help participants
notice tradeoffs in AI use. Their effects are likely to depend on developmental
stage, social context, theological tradition, and whether the practices increase
rather than replace trusted human relationships.

## Proposed Method

- Build a literature-informed taxonomy of Christian practices relevant to AI
  use, drawing on theology, youth development, media studies, and HAI research.
- Co-design research questions and practice prototypes with young adults,
  pastors, educators, and parents; begin with adults age 18 and older.
- Define a theory of change and candidate measures for agency, embodiment,
  relational connection, reflective judgment, and spiritual wellbeing.
- Produce an ethics-reviewed pilot protocol for any later study involving
  minors, with youth assent, parent or guardian consent, and safeguarding.

## Needed Controls

- Do not treat spiritual wellbeing, reduced screen time, or self-reported
  agreement as interchangeable outcomes.
- Avoid collecting sensitive spiritual disclosures or making AI use a condition
  of participation.
- Treat traditions and practices with theological specificity and humility.
- Require independent ethics and safeguarding review before research involving
  minors or vulnerable participants.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This complements `FID-016`, `FID-027`, `FID-035`, and `FID-050`. It focuses on
developing an evidence-ready practice and measurement framework for youth and
young-adult discernment, rather than only cataloging risks, assessing long-term
formation, or studying non-use.

## Outputs

- A taxonomy of Christian practices relevant to AI-era discernment.
- A theory of change and candidate outcome measures.
- Co-design findings and a research-ready pilot protocol.
- A safeguarding and ethics checklist for future youth-facing studies.

## Open Questions

- Which practices support agency without becoming surveillance or moral panic?
- How should outcomes differ for adolescents, emerging adults, families, and
  youth-ministry settings?
- What evidence would justify expanding a pilot into a larger study?

## Ways to Help

- Review the framework from youth ministry, theology, developmental science, education, and HAI research.
- Contribute safeguarding, consent, and research-ethics expertise.
- Help recruit diverse young-adult and practitioner co-design participants.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can identify researchable practice designs and measurement questions.
It cannot establish that any Christian practice will produce a particular
spiritual outcome or provide clinical, pastoral, or child-safety certification.

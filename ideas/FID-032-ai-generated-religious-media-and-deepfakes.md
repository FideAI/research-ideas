# FID-032: AI-Generated Christian Media, Voice, and Deepfakes

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: media ethics, church communications, safety review

## Question

How should churches evaluate and govern AI-generated sermons, testimonies,
devotionals, music, images, video, voice clones, deceased-person simulations, and
synthetic clergy or saint-like personas?

## Why It Matters

AI-generated religious media can educate and inspire, but it can also deceive,
manipulate grief, impersonate trusted leaders, fabricate testimony, or blur the
line between proclamation and synthetic performance.

## Hypothesis

The highest-risk cases involve impersonation, deceased-person simulation,
synthetic testimony, undisclosed generated sermons, and emotionally persuasive
media that appears to carry ecclesial or spiritual authority.

## Proposed Method

- Catalog Christian AI media product patterns.
- Evaluate disclosure, consent, authority claims, emotional manipulation,
  doctrinal fidelity, and risk to grieving or vulnerable audiences.
- Test user understanding of AI-generated vs human-authored religious media.

## Needed Controls

- Consent and likeness rights.
- Disclosure experiments.
- No use of real deceased-person likenesses without permission.
- Distinction between creative illustration and authority simulation.

## Outputs

- Christian AI media risk taxonomy.
- Disclosure and consent guidelines.
- Evaluation protocol for generated religious media.

## Open Questions

- Should churches ever use voice clones of clergy or deceased people?
- What disclosure is sufficient in worship, teaching, or online media?
- How should synthetic testimony be treated?

## Ways to Help

- Review media examples.
- Contribute communications and ethics expertise.
- Design disclosure comprehension studies.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can evaluate media risks and disclosure practices. It cannot make
blanket judgments about all creative uses of AI in Christian media.

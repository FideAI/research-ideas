# FID-033: Faith-Community Data Stewardship and the Common Good

Status: `seed`

Track: `broader-faith-safety`

Primary need: data governance, consent, institutional trust, community benefit

## Question

How should churches, ministries, schools, charities, and faith-facing AI systems
steward community-generated data when it is used for training, fine-tuning,
retrieval, personalization, analytics, institutional memory, or product
improvement?

## Why It Matters

Faith communities generate unusually sensitive data: pastoral notes, prayer
requests, giving records, youth ministry information, counseling-adjacent
records, worship participation, formation histories, and internal deliberations.
If this data is treated only as a private asset or vendor input, communities may
lose control over records that were created in trust for shared spiritual and
institutional goods.

## Hypothesis

Many AI deployments will rely on individual click-through consent or broad
institutional permission even when the relevant data is relational, communal, or
created by vulnerable people. Faith communities will need stronger models of
consent, provenance, deletion, portability, benefit-sharing, and accountable
stewardship than ordinary product analytics provide.

## Proposed Method

- Build a taxonomy of faith-community data types, including pastoral notes,
  prayer requests, education records, donor data, volunteer records, worship
  attendance, sermons, small-group posts, and youth-ministry data.
- Compare consent models: individual consent, institutional consent, community
  consent, opt-out, data trusts, fiduciary stewardship, and purpose-limited use.
- Evaluate deletion, portability, provenance, retention, access control,
  vendor logs, analytics records, third-party references, and benefit-sharing
  requirements for faith-facing AI systems.
- Produce a minimum viable data-stewardship checklist for small churches,
  schools, and ministries before attempting a full governance framework.
- Run stakeholder review with clergy, parents, educators, vulnerable community
  members, technologists, privacy experts, and legal reviewers.

## Needed Controls

- Distinguish public religious content from confidential community records.
- Avoid assuming that institutional permission equals community consent.
- Include minors, vulnerable adults, and people named in records but not account
  holders.
- Treat data about third parties in prayer requests, pastoral notes, and family
  records as a distinct consent and stewardship problem.
- Separate model-training data, retrieval data, analytics data, vendor support
  logs, and operational logs.

## Outputs

- Faith-community data taxonomy.
- Consent and stewardship model comparison.
- Minimum viable data-stewardship checklist for small institutions.
- Vendor and institutional data-governance checklist.
- Field brief on data as a shared good in faith-facing AI.

## Open Questions

- When is individual consent insufficient because data is relational or communal?
- What rights should congregants have to inspect, correct, delete, or export
  faith-community data used by AI systems?
- Can data trusts or fiduciary stewardship models work for small ministries and
  schools?

## Ways to Help

- Contribute examples of sensitive faith-community data workflows.
- Review privacy, consent, and data-governance assumptions.
- Design stewardship rubrics and vendor questions.
- Connect this work to legal, theological, and community-governance expertise.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can propose data-governance tests and stewardship models. It cannot
settle all legal obligations or determine when every faith community may share
or withhold data.

# FID-054: Church Signals of AI-Era Economic Stress

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: church partners, privacy governance, sociology, benevolence and care data

## Question

Can churches and Christian institutions identify privacy-preserving,
non-surveillant signals of AI-era economic stress, such as benevolence requests,
pastoral-care load, job-network activity, counseling referrals, giving changes,
attendance patterns, volunteer availability, or demand for career support?

## Why It Matters

Churches may observe household economic stress before it appears in formal labor
data. But care data is sensitive, relational, and easily misused. Fide AI can
help churches ask what can be learned responsibly without turning pastoral care,
generosity, or community participation into surveillance.

## Hypothesis

Aggregated church-level indicators may help institutions prepare for economic
strain, design support ministries, and understand AI-era disruption. The value
of such indicators depends on strong privacy rules, narrow use cases, explicit
consent where needed, and refusal to rank, target, or profile individuals.

## Proposed Method

- Map church-held data categories relevant to economic stress and care demand.
- Define which categories should never be used, which can be used only with
  consent, and which may be reported as coarse aggregate trends.
- Pilot a privacy-preserving data collection template with churches or
  ministries.
- Compare church-level signals against public economic indicators and local
  qualitative reports.
- Produce a responsible-measurement guide for churches facing AI-era economic
  disruption.

## Needed Controls

- Do not collect personally identifiable pastoral, counseling, benevolence,
  immigration, employment, family, or financial details.
- Avoid donor scoring, congregant profiling, targeted pressure, or automated
  eligibility decisions.
- Separate internal pastoral care from public research claims.
- Include governance review by church leaders, privacy advisors, and affected
  community members.
- Avoid overstating church data as representative of the broader population.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This extends `FID-033` faith-community data and the common good by applying it
to AI-era economic stress, household welfare, and church readiness.

## Outputs

- Church economic-stress signal taxonomy.
- Privacy-preserving aggregate reporting template.
- Data governance checklist for churches and ministries.
- Pilot protocol for local economic-stress measurement.
- Field brief on what churches can and cannot responsibly infer.

## Open Questions

- Which church-level signals are useful without becoming invasive?
- How should churches communicate aggregate findings to members?
- What should be measured locally versus referred to public datasets?
- How can churches preserve discretion and mercy while learning from patterns?

## Ways to Help

- Recruit churches, benevolence teams, pastoral-care teams, and denominational
  leaders for protocol review.
- Contribute privacy, data governance, sociology, or pastoral-care expertise.
- Identify aggregate indicators that are useful but not individually revealing.
- Review failure modes around surveillance, donor pressure, and profiling.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can help churches study aggregate economic-stress signals under strict
privacy constraints. It cannot justify individual surveillance, pastoral
profiling, or automated decisions about care.

# FID-037: AI, Work, Vocation, and Ministry Labor Dignity

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: labor dignity, ministry workflows, education, organizational policy

## Question

How does AI adoption affect the dignity, skill, agency, workload, surveillance
exposure, employment stability, relational quality, and vocational meaning of
people working in churches, schools, ministries, nonprofits, publishing,
translation, counseling-adjacent settings, and mission?

## Why It Matters

AI tools can reduce administrative burdens and expand capacity, but they can
also deskill workers, intensify expectations, automate relational labor, enable
surveillance, weaken formation, and obscure responsibility. Faith institutions
need evidence about how AI changes work and vocation, not only whether a task is
completed faster.

## Hypothesis

The same AI capability may be humane or harmful depending on deployment design.
Tools that augment judgment, protect time for relationships, and preserve skill
can support ministry labor. Tools that monitor, replace, accelerate, or fragment
work may undermine vocational agency and institutional trust even when they
increase output.

## Proposed Method

- Start with a scoped pilot for two roles, such as church administrators and
  Christian educators, before expanding to pastors, youth workers, translators,
  communications staff, volunteers, nonprofit staff, and mission workers.
- Conduct role-based workflow mapping for pastors, teachers, administrators,
  youth workers, translators, communications staff, volunteers, nonprofit staff,
  and mission workers.
- Compare deployment patterns that augment judgment, deskill tasks, intensify
  workloads, monitor workers, replace roles, or automate relational labor.
- Design measures for agency, responsibility, role clarity, skill retention,
  workload, time pressure, surveillance exposure, job insecurity, relational
  time, relational quality, and vocational meaning.
- Produce policy guidance for humane AI adoption in faith institutions.

## Needed Controls

- Include paid staff, volunteers, contractors, and under-resourced institutions.
- Distinguish task automation from role replacement.
- Account for benefits to workers who are currently overburdened.
- Avoid romanticizing inefficient labor when AI can remove genuinely harmful
  administrative load.

## Outputs

- Ministry labor and vocation impact framework.
- Role-based workflow map for AI adoption.
- Worker-centered evaluation survey and interview protocol.
- First-pilot report for one or two ministry roles.
- Institutional policy guidance for humane deployment.

## Open Questions

- Which ministry tasks should be automated, augmented, protected, or never
  delegated to AI?
- How can institutions measure whether AI preserves vocational agency over time?
- What disclosure is owed to staff, volunteers, students, donors, and
  congregants when AI changes human work?

## Ways to Help

- Contribute workflow examples from churches, schools, and nonprofits.
- Review labor, education, and ministry-role assumptions.
- Design worker-impact measures and interview protocols.
- Help translate findings into institutional policy templates.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can evaluate labor and vocation impacts of AI deployments. It cannot
settle employment law questions or decide every institution's staffing choices.

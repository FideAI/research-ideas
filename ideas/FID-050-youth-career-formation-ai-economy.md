# FID-050: Youth Career Formation in an AI Economy

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: educators, youth ministry, career preparation, longitudinal study design

## Question

How is exposure to AI changing how Christian students, parents, educators, and
youth ministries think about career preparation, calling, human value, skill
development, education choices, and hope for the future?

## Why It Matters

Young people are forming expectations about work while AI is changing what
skills appear valuable, which careers feel stable, and what kinds of human
contribution seem durable. Christian schools, parents, churches, and youth
ministries need evidence about how AI affects motivation, vocation, learning,
anxiety, discernment, and preparation for adult life.

## Hypothesis

AI exposure may produce divergent effects. Some students may gain agency by
using AI to learn, explore careers, and imagine new paths. Others may become
more anxious, fatalistic, shortcut-dependent, or confused about which skills are
worth developing. Faith-based education may need to teach both practical AI
literacy and a theological account of work, dignity, and calling.

## Proposed Method

- Survey students, parents, educators, and youth leaders about AI use, career
  expectations, perceived job stability, skill confidence, anxiety, and views of
  vocation.
- Compare students with different levels of AI access, school support, parental
  guidance, and church involvement.
- Run workshops or classroom modules on AI, work, vocation, skill formation,
  and responsible tool use.
- Measure changes in agency, hope, career clarity, learning motivation,
  shortcut behavior, and willingness to develop durable human skills.
- Produce guidance for Christian schools and youth ministries on AI-era career
  and vocation formation.

## Needed Controls

- Follow child-safety, consent, privacy, and school-research requirements.
- Avoid treating anxiety about AI as irrational or treating optimism as
  evidence of preparedness.
- Distinguish AI literacy, career preparation, spiritual formation, and mental
  health support.
- Include students who are not college-bound and students in under-resourced
  school or church settings.
- Avoid using AI to steer minors toward narrow career paths.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This builds on `FID-016` youth religious education and `FID-037` work and
vocation by focusing on career formation, future orientation, and preparation
for an AI-shaped economy.

## Outputs

- Youth AI, vocation, and career-formation survey.
- Workshop or classroom module evaluation protocol.
- Guidance for Christian schools, parents, and youth ministries.
- Field brief on student hope, anxiety, agency, and career preparation.
- Research agenda for longitudinal study of AI-era vocation formation.

## Open Questions

- Which AI skills should every student learn, and which human skills should be
  protected from premature automation?
- How does AI change students' understanding of calling and usefulness?
- What should parents and churches say when students fear their future work
  will not matter?
- How can schools teach AI competence without normalizing shortcuts that weaken
  learning?

## Ways to Help

- Recruit Christian schools, homeschool groups, youth ministries, and parent
  networks.
- Review student survey items and classroom module designs.
- Contribute education, child-development, youth-ministry, or labor-market
  expertise.
- Help design privacy-preserving longitudinal follow-up.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can study how students and families think about work, vocation, and
AI-era preparation in defined settings. It cannot predict individual career
outcomes or replace professional educational and counseling guidance.

# FID-035: Digital Sobriety and AI Non-Use Recommendations

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: formation, education, user agency, attention, pastoral boundaries

## Question

Can faith-facing AI systems recognize when the best response is not more AI
assistance, but silence, prayer, study, human counsel, embodied community,
family conversation, teacher engagement, pastoral care, or delayed action?

## Why It Matters

Most AI evaluation rewards systems for answering well. Faith-facing systems also
need to know when answering too quickly or too completely can displace practices
of discernment, learning, attention, responsibility, repentance, and community.
Digital sobriety asks whether systems can preserve human agency and spiritual
formation by recommending limits on their own use.

## Hypothesis

AI systems will tend to over-serve because direct assistance looks helpful in
short-term user satisfaction. They may draft the prayer, sermon, apology,
homework answer, discernment plan, or pastoral message when the formative good
would be to slow down, involve another person, or engage a non-digital practice.

## Proposed Method

- Build scenarios involving homework shortcuts, sermon shortcuts, prayer
  outsourcing, grief support, repeated reassurance-seeking, vocational
  discernment, youth questions, moral decisions, and pastoral-adjacent requests.
- Compare whether models answer directly, over-serve, redirect, recommend human
  support, or propose non-AI practices.
- Measure user understanding, perceived helpfulness, trust, frustration, and
  willingness to accept restraint.
- Score concrete non-use outcomes: answered directly when it should have
  redirected, refused when it should have helped, recommended human counsel while
  giving safe interim support, or encouraged offline practice without guilt or
  manipulation.
- Develop a digital-sobriety rubric distinct from generic refusal and safety
  rubrics.

## Needed Controls

- Avoid turning every hard question into a refusal.
- Distinguish healthy restraint from evasive non-answering.
- Include users who genuinely lack access to safe human support.
- Account for differences among prayer, education, pastoral care, mental health,
  and ordinary administration.

## Outputs

- Digital-sobriety evaluation rubric.
- Scenario set for AI non-use and self-limitation.
- User-study protocol for helpful restraint.
- Non-use design pattern library for faith-facing products.
- Guidance for faith-facing product design and disclosure.

## Open Questions

- What kinds of AI non-use recommendations do users find trustworthy rather than
  patronizing?
- When should a system redirect to human community even if it can answer safely?
- How can systems recommend prayer, study, or pastoral care without simulating
  spiritual authority?

## Ways to Help

- Draft scenarios where AI assistance may undermine formation.
- Design user studies on restraint, agency, and trust.
- Review theological, educational, youth-safety, and pastoral boundaries.
- Build product patterns for recommending non-AI practices.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can evaluate whether systems recommend appropriate limits on their own
use. It cannot determine a universal rule for when every person should or should
not use AI.

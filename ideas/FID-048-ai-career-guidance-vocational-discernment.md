# FID-048: AI Career Guidance and Vocational Discernment

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: career guidance, pastoral theology, HAI research, product evaluation

## Question

Can AI tools help people facing career disruption explore skills, training,
applications, finances, and next steps while preserving vocational agency,
human counsel, realistic constraints, and a richer account of calling than
market optimization?

## Why It Matters

Workers displaced or unsettled by AI may turn to AI systems for career advice,
resume drafting, job matching, retraining plans, and emotional reassurance.
These tools can reduce search costs and widen access to guidance. They can also
overpromise, flatten vocation into income maximization, isolate users from
human support, or provide confident advice without knowing local constraints.

## Hypothesis

AI career guidance will be most helpful when it acts as a structured aid to
discernment: clarifying options, translating skills, identifying constraints,
encouraging human counsel, and supporting concrete next steps. It will be
riskier when it becomes a private authority for identity, calling, risk-taking,
or major household decisions.

## Proposed Method

- Build scenario tests for displaced workers, students, older workers,
  caregivers, ministry staff, non-college workers, and people considering
  retraining.
- Compare AI responses on skill translation, job-search planning, training
  advice, financial realism, dignity, hope, human referral, and avoidance of
  false certainty.
- Run user studies where participants evaluate whether AI advice improves
  clarity, agency, confidence, and willingness to seek human support.
- Include church and career-ministry reviewers to assess whether advice respects
  vocation, family obligations, local context, and moral constraints.
- Develop guidance for churches that want to use AI-assisted career support
  without outsourcing vocational discernment.

## Needed Controls

- Avoid unsafe financial, legal, immigration, mental-health, or employment-law
  advice.
- Account for users with limited education, limited internet access, disability,
  caregiving responsibilities, rural constraints, or language barriers.
- Distinguish helpful encouragement from unrealistic optimism.
- Require disclosure when AI cannot verify labor-market demand, credential
  value, costs, or local opportunities.
- Avoid making pastors or volunteers responsible for technical career advice
  outside their competence.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This connects `FID-037` on work and vocation with `FID-035` on digital sobriety:
some career questions call for AI assistance, while others call for slower human
discernment and accountable counsel.

## Outputs

- AI career-guidance and vocation evaluation rubric.
- Scenario suite for job disruption, retraining, and discernment.
- User-study protocol for clarity, agency, and support-seeking.
- Church guide for AI-assisted career support.
- Failure taxonomy for overconfident, atomizing, or market-reductive advice.

## Open Questions

- When does AI career guidance become too directive?
- How should systems handle users who feel their previous work has lost meaning?
- What does good referral to human counsel look like in churches with limited
  career-support capacity?
- Which outcomes matter most: faster applications, better matches, reduced
  anxiety, stronger agency, or deeper vocational clarity?

## Ways to Help

- Draft realistic worker, student, and household transition scenarios.
- Review career guidance for financial, legal, pastoral, and educational risks.
- Design user studies that measure agency rather than short-term satisfaction
  alone.
- Contribute church-based career ministry or workforce-development experience.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can evaluate AI career-guidance behavior and support patterns under
defined conditions. It cannot certify career advice, guarantee employment
outcomes, or replace qualified human counsel.

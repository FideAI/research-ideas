# FID-024: Faith-AI Incident Database

Status: `seed`

Track: `broader-faith-safety`

Primary need: taxonomy design, reporting governance

## Question

Should Fide AI maintain a public or semi-public incident database for failures,
near misses, misuse, and disputed deployments of AI in faith-facing contexts?

## Why It Matters

Safety fields learn from incidents. Faith communities are already encountering
AI-generated sermons, spiritual chatbots, pastoral automation, synthetic clergy,
and religious misinformation. Without a shared incident vocabulary, institutions
will repeat mistakes.

## Hypothesis

A carefully governed incident database can reveal recurring patterns and improve
standards, but only if it protects privacy, avoids sensationalism, distinguishes
verified from reported incidents, and gives organizations a correction process.

## Proposed Method

- Define incident categories and severity levels.
- Create intake, verification, redaction, and publication workflows.
- Track source type, affected population, system type, failure mode, evidence
  quality, and remediation.
- Publish aggregate trend reports before publishing sensitive individual cases.

## Needed Controls

- Defamation and privacy review.
- Evidence-quality labels.
- Right-of-reply and correction process.
- Redaction of vulnerable-user details.

## Outputs

- Incident taxonomy.
- Intake form.
- Public aggregate report template.
- Governance policy for incident publication.

## Open Questions

- Should the database be public, private, or tiered?
- What threshold makes a report publishable?
- How should anonymous reports be handled?

## Ways to Help

- Design taxonomy and severity levels.
- Review legal/privacy risks.
- Contribute incident-response experience.
- Build intake and redaction tooling.

## Public Claim Boundary

An incident database can document reported and verified patterns. It should not
be treated as a complete census of faith-AI harm or as adjudication of theology.

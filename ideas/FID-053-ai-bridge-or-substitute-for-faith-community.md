# FID-053: AI as Bridge or Substitute for Faith Community

Status: `seed`

Track: `broader-faith-safety`

Primary need: sociology of religion, pastoral care, UX research, aggregate and field measurement

## Question

When people use AI for religious, spiritual, vocational, or meaning-related
questions during economic disruption, does that use tend to bridge them toward
churches, clergy, mentors, family, support groups, and local institutions, or
substitute for embodied community?

## Why It Matters

AI can help people name questions they were afraid to ask, find a church, draft
an email to a pastor, prepare for a hard conversation, or locate practical
support. It can also become a private spiritual interface that absorbs distress
without connecting people to accountable human care. Churches need evidence
about both possibilities.

## Hypothesis

Bridge and substitution patterns will depend on product design, user trust,
prior religious background, local community access, economic vulnerability, and
the type of question being asked. Systems that offer accurate source grounding,
non-coercive referral, and bounded support may function as bridges. Systems that
simulate authority, companionship, or certainty may increase substitution risk.

## Proposed Method

- Define bridge indicators, such as seeking a church, contacting clergy,
  discussing with family, joining a group, using a benevolence resource, or
  asking how to talk to a human.
- Define substitution indicators, such as secrecy, exclusive reliance,
  repeated reassurance-seeking, simulated spiritual authority, avoidance of
  human disclosure, or attachment to AI as primary counsel.
- Study aggregate provider-side patterns where privacy-safe category analysis is
  available.
- Conduct consented field interviews with churches, pastors, and users who have
  used AI for religious or meaning-related questions.
- Compare product patterns that recommend human support, offer non-use
  guidance, or continue extended private spiritual dialogue.

## Needed Controls

- Do not assume every person should be routed to a church or religious leader.
- Include users with no safe local community, religious trauma, abuse risk, or
  minority-faith constraints.
- Avoid treating one-time AI use as substitution.
- Separate healthy private reflection from avoidant isolation.
- Do not infer individual religious identity or community participation from
  aggregate signals.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This extends `FID-004` relational substitution risk and `FID-042` clergy and
spiritual-leader referral parity by focusing on economic disruption and
meaning-seeking as a context where bridge and substitution dynamics may be
especially visible.

## Outputs

- Bridge-versus-substitution measurement framework.
- Privacy-preserving aggregate signal design.
- Interview protocol for users, pastors, and community-support leaders.
- Product pattern library for non-coercive referral to human support.
- Church-facing field brief on AI, spiritual search, and embodied community.

## Open Questions

- What evidence would distinguish bridge behavior from substitution behavior?
- Which users are helped by AI-mediated rehearsal before contacting a person?
- When is referral to clergy appropriate alongside therapy, workforce, legal,
  emergency, or family support?
- How should churches respond to people who first explored faith or purpose
  through AI?

## Ways to Help

- Contribute sociology, pastoral care, UX, or measurement expertise.
- Draft bridge and substitution indicators that preserve user dignity.
- Recruit churches and community institutions for field interviews.
- Review referral patterns for religious and nonreligious users.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can evaluate bridge and substitution patterns under defined
conditions. It cannot determine whether any individual user's AI use is
spiritually healthy or whether a person should join a particular community.

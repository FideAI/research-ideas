# FID-049: Unequal AI Access Through Faith Communities

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: access research, community partners, digital literacy, equity

## Question

Are churches and faith-based institutions helping under-resourced workers,
families, students, older adults, immigrants, rural communities, and small
nonprofits access useful AI tools, or are AI benefits concentrating among people
who already have technical, educational, financial, and institutional advantage?

## Why It Matters

AI may widen inequality if the people most able to benefit already have better
devices, broadband, English fluency, paid tools, institutional support, and
confidence using technology. Churches often serve people outside elite
knowledge-work settings and may become trusted places for AI literacy, access,
discernment, and caution. That role needs evidence rather than assumption.

## Hypothesis

Faith communities could reduce AI access gaps when they provide trusted
training, shared infrastructure, bilingual support, safe experimentation,
accountability, and practical use cases. They could also unintentionally widen
gaps if AI programs only serve already-confident users or promote tools without
privacy, cost, safety, or labor-market realism.

## Proposed Method

- Survey AI access, usage, trust, concerns, and practical needs among church
  members, ministry participants, parents, students, older adults, and staff.
- Compare barriers such as cost, device access, language, disability, digital
  confidence, privacy concern, theological concern, and lack of relevant use
  cases.
- Pilot lightweight AI literacy or career-support workshops through churches or
  Christian nonprofits.
- Measure changes in practical capability, confidence, privacy understanding,
  support-seeking, and appropriate non-use.
- Produce guidance for churches deciding whether and how to offer AI access or
  literacy programs.

## Needed Controls

- Do not assume AI access is always beneficial; include reasons for prudent
  non-use or delayed adoption.
- Avoid collecting sensitive spiritual, immigration, employment, or household
  data unnecessarily.
- Include people with low trust in AI, low digital confidence, or limited
  English fluency.
- Distinguish access to tools from meaningful benefit.
- Avoid vendor promotion or product endorsement through church channels.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This extends `FID-020` community co-design and `FID-037` work and vocation by
asking who benefits from AI access and whether churches can reduce or amplify
adoption gaps.

## Outputs

- AI access and benefit survey for faith communities.
- Barrier taxonomy for under-resourced church and ministry contexts.
- Pilot workshop protocol for AI literacy and responsible use.
- Field brief on unequal access, benefit, and non-use.
- Church guidance for vendor-neutral AI literacy programming.

## Open Questions

- Which AI uses are most valuable for under-resourced households and workers?
- What forms of AI training build agency without dependency or hype?
- How should churches handle paid tools, data privacy, and member vulnerability?
- When is the right intervention not access, but protection from exploitative or
  inappropriate AI use?

## Ways to Help

- Recruit churches, schools, ministries, or nonprofits serving under-resourced
  communities.
- Translate surveys and workshop materials.
- Review accessibility, privacy, and digital-literacy assumptions.
- Design outcome measures for real benefit rather than mere tool adoption.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can study AI access, barriers, and benefits in defined faith-community
settings. It cannot claim that AI access is uniformly good or that churches can
solve structural inequality by offering tool training.

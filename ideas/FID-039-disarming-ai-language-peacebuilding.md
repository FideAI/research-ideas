# FID-039: Disarming AI Language in Faith, Conflict, and Peacebuilding

Status: `seed`

Track: `broader-faith-safety`

Primary need: conflict communication, peacebuilding, moderation, pastoral leadership

## Question

Can AI systems preserve truth, moral clarity, and victim protection while
reducing contempt, humiliation, factional capture, scapegoating, and
violence-normalizing rhetoric in polarized religious, congregational, political,
interfaith, and social-conflict contexts?

## Why It Matters

Faith communities often turn to language during conflict: sermons, statements,
moderation decisions, counseling, interfaith dialogue, public advocacy, and
private messages. AI systems can inflame conflict by sharpening factional
identity or can flatten conflict by asking victims and whistleblowers to accept
false peace. Fide AI can test whether systems help disarm words without
disarming truth.

## Hypothesis

Generic civility metrics will not be enough. Models may reward bland neutrality,
conflict avoidance, or reputation protection while missing the need for
truth-telling, repentance, justice, and repair. A better benchmark should test
for de-escalation that remains specific, accountable, and attentive to people
harmed by conflict.

## Proposed Method

- Create multi-turn scenarios involving church splits, political sermons,
  interfaith conflict, social-media outrage, migration, war, abuse
  accountability, racial injustice, and denominational disputes.
- Score for truthfulness, courage, specificity, contempt, scapegoating,
  humiliation, collective blame, factual precision, non-humiliation,
  affected-party perspective, de-escalation, repair orientation, and resistance
  to violence-normalizing rhetoric.
- Test whether models distinguish peacebuilding from conflict avoidance, false
  peace, appeasement, or institutional reputation management.
- Test both chat responses and drafted artifacts such as sermons, statements,
  moderation notes, emails, and social-media posts.
- Involve pastors, peacebuilding practitioners, trauma-informed reviewers,
  journalists, theologians, and community leaders.

## Needed Controls

- Do not equate strong moral language with harmful escalation.
- Include cases where silence or neutrality protects the powerful.
- Avoid pressuring victims to reconcile prematurely.
- Separate moderation, pastoral response, public advocacy, and private counsel.

## Outputs

- Faith-conflict and peacebuilding evaluation suite.
- Disarming-language rubric for AI communication.
- False-peace failure taxonomy.
- Red-team prompts for factional capture and scapegoating.
- Product guidance for conflict-sensitive AI features.

## Open Questions

- How can AI systems name injustice without escalating contempt or collective
  blame?
- What counts as repair-oriented language across different Christian and
  interfaith contexts?
- How should models respond when users request rhetorically effective but
  dehumanizing messages?

## Ways to Help

- Contribute conflict and peacebuilding scenarios.
- Review rubrics for de-escalation, truthfulness, and affected-party care.
- Build multilingual and cross-cultural examples.
- Connect practitioners who work in mediation, trauma care, and public theology.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can evaluate conflict-sensitive language patterns. It cannot certify
that an AI system produces reconciliation or replace trained mediators, pastors,
legal processes, or community leadership.

# FID-047: Churches as Labor-Transition Support Institutions

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: institutional partners, labor transition, social support, field research

## Question

What roles do churches, Christian nonprofits, schools, and ministries play when
workers and households face AI-related layoffs, role disruption, retraining
needs, income instability, or loss of vocational direction?

## Why It Matters

Churches are often local support institutions before they are formal workforce
programs. They provide pastoral care, benevolence funds, informal job networks,
childcare support, meals, prayer, teaching, community, referrals, and trusted
interpretation of confusing social change. If AI meaningfully disrupts work,
churches may become part of the response whether or not they have prepared for
that role.

## Hypothesis

Churches already provide labor-transition support, but much of it is informal,
uneven, and unmeasured. Mapping these practices could identify where churches
help households absorb economic shocks, where they are underprepared, and where
AI tools or external partnerships could responsibly expand capacity.

## Proposed Method

- Interview pastors, deacons, benevolence teams, career-ministry leaders,
  school leaders, nonprofit staff, and congregants affected by labor disruption.
- Map existing church responses: job referrals, financial aid, counseling,
  retraining referrals, resume help, childcare, meals, pastoral care, sermons,
  classes, and small-group support.
- Compare churches by size, geography, socioeconomic context, denomination, and
  existing workforce partnerships.
- Identify gaps between what displaced workers need and what churches are
  prepared to provide.
- Develop a readiness framework for churches responding to AI labor disruption.

## Needed Controls

- Avoid treating churches as substitutes for employers, government benefits,
  unions, professional counseling, or workforce agencies.
- Distinguish emergency care, pastoral care, career support, financial support,
  and long-term economic mobility.
- Include under-resourced churches that may face the highest need and the
  lowest capacity.
- Protect confidential benevolence, counseling, employment, and household data.
- Avoid implying that every church should run a workforce program.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This complements `FID-037` by studying churches as support infrastructure for
workers and households, not only as employers or adopters of AI tools.

## Outputs

- Typology of church labor-transition support.
- Interview guide for pastors, staff, volunteers, and affected workers.
- Church readiness framework for AI-related work disruption.
- Field brief on gaps, risks, and promising practices.
- Partner map for workforce agencies, schools, charities, and ministries.

## Open Questions

- What support do churches provide uniquely well, and what should be referred
  elsewhere?
- How do churches know when economic distress has become pastoral, family, or
  mental-health distress?
- Which models work for small churches without dedicated staff?
- How can churches avoid shaming, politicizing, or overspiritualizing job loss?

## Ways to Help

- Share examples of job-transition, benevolence, or career-support ministries.
- Help recruit churches and Christian nonprofits for qualitative interviews.
- Review readiness criteria from pastoral, workforce, legal, and social-service
  perspectives.
- Identify partnerships that preserve church mission while expanding practical
  support.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can document and evaluate church labor-transition support practices.
It cannot claim churches are sufficient replacements for public policy,
employer responsibility, or professional workforce services.

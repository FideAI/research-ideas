# FID-046: AI Displacement, Meaning, and Vocation

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: labor economics, pastoral care, vocation, survey design

## Question

How do people affected by AI-related job loss, role disruption, workplace
automation, or career uncertainty experience changes in purpose, dignity,
vocation, anxiety, household decision-making, and participation in church or
community life?

## Why It Matters

AI labor disruption is not only a labor-market event. People often experience
job loss or occupational instability as a loss of role, future, identity,
status, community, and meaning. Churches regularly encounter these burdens
through pastoral care, benevolence, counseling referrals, small groups, job
networks, family stress, youth formation, and teaching about work and calling.

## Hypothesis

Standard labor-market measures will miss important welfare effects if they only
track employment, wages, reskilling, or productivity. Measures of meaning,
agency, social support, spiritual distress, perceived dignity, and vocational
hope may help explain why some workers recover, retrain, seek support, or
withdraw after AI-related disruption.

## Proposed Method

- Develop a survey and interview protocol for workers experiencing AI-related
  job insecurity, layoffs, task automation, role redesign, or career anxiety.
- Measure labor outcomes alongside purpose, vocation, dignity, anxiety, social
  support, church participation, household stress, and willingness to retrain.
- Compare workers with strong local support networks to workers navigating
  disruption more privately or primarily through online tools.
- Include pastoral-care and ministry-provider interviews to understand what
  churches are already seeing in displaced or anxious workers.
- Produce a church-facing field brief on how AI labor disruption is showing up
  as meaning, care, and household-welfare pressure.

## Needed Controls

- Avoid attributing every layoff or career anxiety to AI without evidence.
- Distinguish actual displacement, anticipated displacement, job redesign,
  status loss, and generalized technology anxiety.
- Protect participants from public exposure of employment, financial, or
  pastoral-care details.
- Include non-church comparison data where possible rather than assuming church
  participation is always protective.
- Avoid reducing vocation to job-market success or retraining compliance.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This extends `FID-037` by focusing specifically on people experiencing labor
disruption and meaning loss, rather than on AI adoption inside ministry work
itself.

## Outputs

- AI displacement and vocation survey instrument.
- Interview protocol for workers and pastoral-care providers.
- Field brief on meaning, dignity, and household welfare under AI disruption.
- Measurement memo connecting labor-market outcomes to vocation and support.
- Practical church guide for recognizing and responding to AI-related work
  disruption.

## Open Questions

- Which indicators best distinguish ordinary career stress from AI-related
  disruption?
- How should churches discuss vocation when some forms of work are being
  automated or devalued?
- What support helps people preserve agency and hope without offering shallow
  reassurance?
- How can research include people outside formal church membership who still
  seek help from faith communities?

## Ways to Help

- Contribute labor-economics, sociology, psychology, or pastoral-care expertise.
- Help design survey measures for meaning, dignity, vocation, and agency.
- Recruit churches, ministries, or workforce-support organizations for pilot
  interviews.
- Review protocols for privacy, consent, and participant vulnerability.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can study how AI-related labor disruption is experienced and supported
in defined communities. It cannot prove that AI caused every reported job loss
or prescribe a universal theology of work.

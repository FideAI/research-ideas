# FID-065: AI, Local Knowledge, and Faith-Based Civil Society

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: civil-society research, organizational studies, political economy, faith-based partners

## Question

How do AI tools affect the ability of churches, ministries, faith-based
nonprofits, and faith-rooted social enterprises to discover, steward, and act
on local knowledge? Which deployment conditions expand their capacity for local
problem solving, and which conditions make them dependent on distant vendors,
generic data, or externally imposed priorities?

## Why It Matters

Faith-based organizations often respond to needs that are difficult to see in
centralized datasets: household stress, informal care networks, neighborhood
relationships, vocational barriers, and local sources of trust. AI may reduce
administrative burden and extend capacity. It may also erase context, extract
sensitive information, or shift institutional judgment to systems whose
incentives and assumptions are outside the community's control.

## Hypothesis

AI will strengthen local problem-solving capacity only when organizations retain
contextual judgment, usable data governance, realistic exit options, and the
ability to adapt tools to local workflows. Convenience without those conditions
will tend to centralize expertise and weaken institutional learning.

## Proposed Method

- Develop a framework for local knowledge in faith-based civil society,
  including context capture, consent, institutional memory, adaptation,
  portability, and community accountability.
- Conduct comparative case studies and interviews across churches, ministries,
  charities, and faith-rooted social enterprises using or considering AI.
- Survey organizational leaders about capacity, dependence, procurement,
  workflow change, and perceived effects on local problem solving.
- Produce a field-ready study design for evaluating AI-supported programs
  without collecting unnecessary personal or pastoral data.

## Needed Controls

- Do not assume local knowledge is complete, unbiased, or always safe to use.
- Protect sensitive pastoral, beneficiary, employment, and household data.
- Distinguish organizational capability from theological authority or quality of
  care.
- Include small organizations and resource-constrained communities, not only
  well-staffed institutions.
- Do not prescribe political or regulatory outcomes.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This complements `FID-037`, `FID-040`, `FID-047`, and `FID-049`. Its focus is
not individual work or a general governance scorecard, but the empirical
conditions under which faith-based associations retain local knowledge and
institutional agency while using AI.

## Outputs

- A research framework for AI and local knowledge in faith-based civil society.
- Comparative case-study protocol and organizational survey instrument.
- Data-governance and institutional-learning indicators.
- A public field brief on capacity, dependence, and responsible local adoption.

## Open Questions

- What local knowledge should never be entered into an external AI system?
- How can institutions share beneficial practices without exposing vulnerable
  people or making community knowledge extractable?
- Which procurement terms most affect practical local agency?

## Ways to Help

- Connect researchers with churches, ministries, nonprofits, and social enterprises willing to inform the study.
- Review the constructs from organizational studies, economics, privacy, and Christian social thought.
- Help design privacy-preserving survey and case-study methods.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can assess organizational conditions associated with local capacity
and dependence. It cannot determine whether any faith-based organization should
adopt AI or prove that a particular market or governance structure is best.

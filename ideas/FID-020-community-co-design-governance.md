# FID-020: Community Co-Design and Faith-AI Governance

Status: `seed`

Track: `broader-faith-safety`

Primary need: participatory design, institutional partners

## Question

How should faith communities participate in defining, testing, and governing AI
systems that affect worship, education, pastoral care, administration, and moral
formation?

## Why It Matters

Generic AI ethics frameworks often treat communities as deployment sites rather
than co-design partners. Faith communities have internal authority structures,
theological commitments, pastoral practices, and accountability norms that should
shape evaluation.

## Hypothesis

Co-designed evaluations will identify risks and success criteria that generic
AI-safety rubrics miss, especially around authority, ritual, formation,
community trust, and spiritual dependency.

## Proposed Method

- Run structured workshops with faith institutions and community members.
- Convert community concerns into evaluation dimensions and scenario cards.
- Compare co-designed rubrics against generic helpfulness/safety/factuality
  rubrics.
- Document governance patterns for responsible deployment.

## Needed Controls

- Avoid treating one institution as representative of a whole tradition.
- Protect private community concerns.
- Manage institutional conflicts of interest.
- Separate research participation from product endorsement.

## Outputs

- Co-design workshop protocol.
- Community-derived risk taxonomy.
- Governance guide for faith institutions.
- Comparative report on generic vs community-derived evaluation criteria.

## Open Questions

- Who legitimately represents a community for evaluation design?
- How should dissenting voices inside institutions be included?
- What parts of community input should remain private?

## Ways to Help

- Host a workshop.
- Facilitate participatory design.
- Translate concerns into measurable evaluation criteria.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can document community-derived criteria. It should not claim consensus
across a tradition or endorsement by participants without explicit approval.

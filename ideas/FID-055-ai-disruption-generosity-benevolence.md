# FID-055: AI Disruption, Generosity, and Benevolence

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: church finance, household welfare, philanthropy, economic sociology

## Question

How does AI-related job uncertainty, income disruption, career anxiety, or
economic optimism affect church giving, generosity, benevolence demand, mutual
aid, missions support, and household willingness to help others?

## Why It Matters

Economic disruption affects both sides of church care: households may have less
capacity to give, while more people may need help. AI may also change how people
think about abundance, scarcity, stewardship, obligation, risk, and future
security. Churches need evidence for budgeting, benevolence planning, teaching,
and mutual-aid design.

## Hypothesis

AI-related economic disruption may produce uneven effects. Some households may
reduce giving because of fear or income loss. Others may increase generosity in
response to visible need. Churches may experience simultaneous pressure on
revenues, benevolence requests, staff capacity, and community-care expectations.

## Proposed Method

- Survey households about AI-related economic expectations, generosity, giving
  intentions, benevolence attitudes, and mutual-aid participation.
- Interview church finance teams, benevolence teams, pastors, and nonprofit
  partners about observed changes in giving and care demand.
- Analyze privacy-preserving aggregate church finance and benevolence trends
  where institutions consent.
- Compare local trends against public economic indicators, layoff data, and
  industry exposure measures.
- Produce church guidance for stewardship, benevolence, and mutual-aid planning
  during AI-era economic uncertainty.

## Needed Controls

- Do not expose household giving, debt, benevolence, employment, or pastoral
  care details.
- Distinguish actual income loss from anticipated risk or generalized anxiety.
- Avoid using research to pressure giving or stigmatize aid recipients.
- Include smaller and lower-income churches, not only large institutions with
  clean financial systems.
- Separate descriptive findings from theological claims about generosity.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This complements `FID-047` by focusing on the financial and mutual-aid dynamics
of church response to labor disruption.

## Outputs

- Household survey on AI uncertainty, generosity, and benevolence.
- Church finance and benevolence interview protocol.
- Privacy-safe aggregate measurement template.
- Field brief on generosity and care demand under AI disruption.
- Stewardship and benevolence planning guide for churches.

## Open Questions

- How quickly do giving and benevolence patterns respond to AI-related economic
  shocks?
- Do sermons or church teaching on work and generosity affect household
  responses to uncertainty?
- What mutual-aid models preserve dignity during job disruption?
- How should churches budget when economic optimism and anxiety coexist?

## Ways to Help

- Recruit churches willing to review or pilot privacy-safe aggregate measures.
- Contribute church finance, philanthropy, household welfare, or sociology
  expertise.
- Help design survey items that do not shame respondents or aid recipients.
- Identify public economic indicators suitable for comparison.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can study aggregate patterns in generosity, benevolence, and economic
stress. It cannot expose individual giving or aid records, pressure
congregants, or settle theological claims about stewardship.

# FID-052: AI as Private First Counsel During Economic Stress

Status: `seed`

Track: `broader-faith-safety`

Primary need: pastoral care, HAI research, mental health boundaries, privacy-preserving measurement

## Question

When people experience job loss, financial stress, career uncertainty, or
automation anxiety, do they turn to AI systems as a private first place for
emotional, spiritual, moral, or vocational counsel before seeking help from
family, churches, pastors, counselors, mentors, or workforce institutions?

## Why It Matters

AI systems can lower the cost of disclosing fear, shame, confusion, or doubt.
That may help people rehearse hard conversations and find resources. It may also
create a private substitute for embodied support at moments when people need
accountable counsel, practical aid, safeguarding, or community.

## Hypothesis

During economic stress, some users will use AI as a low-friction first counsel
for questions that combine practical, emotional, moral, and spiritual needs.
Good systems may help users prepare for human support and concrete action. Poor
systems may overvalidate, overpersonalize, delay escalation, or make users more
isolated.

## Proposed Method

- Develop privacy-safe categories for economic stress, emotional disclosure,
  spiritual support, moral decision-making, and human-referral behavior.
- Analyze aggregate provider-side trends where available, without receiving raw
  conversations.
- Run consented user studies or diaries with people navigating career or
  financial disruption.
- Compare whether AI guidance encourages users toward appropriate human support,
  local resources, churches, workforce agencies, counseling, or emergency paths.
- Create scenario tests for AI systems responding to economically stressed users
  who ask for spiritual certainty, secrecy, reassurance, or major life advice.

## Needed Controls

- Treat mental-health, spiritual, financial, and employment distress as
  sensitive data.
- Do not present pastors, churches, or AI tools as substitutes for licensed
  care, legal advice, emergency response, or workforce services.
- Include users who have been harmed by religious communities or lack safe local
  support.
- Separate ordinary reflection from crisis, coercion, abuse, self-harm, or
  financial emergency cases.
- Avoid measuring "more referral" as always better; referrals must be relevant,
  safe, and non-coercive.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This connects `FID-003`, `FID-004`, `FID-010`, `FID-018`, and `FID-048` by
asking whether economic stress changes the role AI plays as counsel, companion,
or bridge to human support.

## Outputs

- First-counsel taxonomy for AI use under economic stress.
- Scenario suite for job loss, financial fear, vocation, and spiritual support.
- User-study protocol for disclosure, isolation, agency, and referral.
- Field brief for churches on AI-mediated first disclosure.
- Design guidance for bridging users toward appropriate human support.

## Open Questions

- When is private AI reflection beneficial preparation for human counsel?
- When does AI-mediated reassurance delay necessary disclosure or support?
- What referral language is appropriate for users who do not trust churches or
  local institutions?
- How can providers measure first-counsel patterns without exposing private
  distress?

## Ways to Help

- Review scenario designs from pastoral, clinical, legal, and workforce-support
  perspectives.
- Help define privacy-safe measures of referral and isolation.
- Recruit consented study participants through churches or community partners.
- Contribute HAI research methods for studying disclosure and support-seeking.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can study support-seeking patterns under defined conditions. It cannot
diagnose users, evaluate individual spiritual health, or treat AI as a pastor,
counselor, lawyer, or emergency responder.

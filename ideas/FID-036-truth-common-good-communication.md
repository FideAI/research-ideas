# FID-036: Truth as a Common Good in Christian AI Communication

Status: `seed`

Track: `christian-church`

Primary need: communication integrity, institutional trust, journalism, crisis response

## Question

How do AI tools used for summarization, search, moderation, recommendation,
crisis communication, public statements, and internal updates affect a Christian
community's shared grasp of facts, uncertainty, accountability, and trust?

## Why It Matters

Christian communities depend on truthful communication in ordinary teaching,
public witness, abuse response, denominational conflict, financial stewardship,
local disputes, and institutional crisis. AI systems can help people understand
complex information, but they can also launder reputations, flatten contested
facts, intensify factions, bury inconvenient sources, or make false consensus
feel authoritative.

## Hypothesis

The greatest risks will appear when AI systems summarize disputed events,
recommend sources, draft public statements, moderate community discussion, or
translate uncertainty into confident institutional language. Systems that sound
balanced may still damage the truth commons if they hide source provenance,
collapse facts into interpretations, or optimize for reputational calm.

## Proposed Method

- Create cases involving contested church news, abuse allegations,
  denominational conflict, financial controversy, theological disputes,
  social-media rumors, crisis statements, and pastoral public communication.
- Test whether systems preserve uncertainty, cite primary sources, avoid
  reputation laundering, resist factional framing, and distinguish fact from
  interpretation.
- Evaluate disclosure, source traceability, correction workflows, correction
  latency, correction propagation, contestability, and affected-party
  perspectives.
- Involve journalists, church communicators, abuse-prevention experts, pastors,
  theologians, lay stakeholders, and institutional leaders.

## Needed Controls

- Use synthetic cases by default and carefully redacted cases only with
  appropriate consent, legal review, and abuse-prevention review.
- Distinguish privacy protection from reputation management.
- Avoid rewarding inflammatory certainty over truthful uncertainty.
- Avoid optimizing for institutional calm when truthful accountability requires
  discomfort, correction, or public acknowledgment.
- Include both institutional and harmed-party perspectives.

## Outputs

- Truth-commons evaluation rubric for Christian communication.
- Crisis and contested-fact scenario set.
- Source-traceability and correction-workflow guidance.
- Field brief for church communicators and AI product teams.

## Open Questions

- How should AI systems represent unresolved allegations, contested facts, and
  confidential information?
- What correction and appeal workflows are needed when AI summaries shape a
  community's understanding?
- How can systems support peace without suppressing truth or accountability?

## Ways to Help

- Contribute communication, journalism, and crisis-response scenarios.
- Review rubrics for factuality, uncertainty, and source provenance.
- Design tests for reputation laundering and factional framing.
- Connect affected-community perspectives to evaluation design.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can evaluate communication risks and truth-preserving workflows. It
cannot adjudicate specific institutional controversies or replace investigative,
pastoral, legal, or journalistic judgment.

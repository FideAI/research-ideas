# FID-040: AI Power Concentration and Subsidiarity Benchmarks

Status: `seed`

Track: `broader-faith-safety`

Primary need: AI governance, institutional agency, community oversight, platform accountability

## Question

How can Fide AI evaluate whether AI systems and governance arrangements preserve
meaningful local agency for churches, schools, ministries, nonprofits, families,
and vulnerable communities rather than concentrating power in vendors, platforms,
central institutions, or distant technical experts?

## Why It Matters

Faith communities may become dependent on AI systems they cannot inspect,
contest, configure, exit, or govern. Even helpful systems can weaken local
responsibility if important norms are set only by platform defaults, proprietary
models, centralized policy teams, or opaque institutional contracts. Subsidiarity
turns this into a measurable question of community agency and accountability.

## Hypothesis

AI deployments will often offer user-friendly customization without meaningful
community control. Stronger governance will require measurable indicators such
as local configuration, appeal, audit access, data portability, policy
transparency, participatory standard-setting, vendor-switching feasibility,
local override mechanisms, and safeguards against capture by abusive local
leaders.

## Proposed Method

- Define subsidiarity indicators: local configuration, meaningful appeal,
  community audit access, data portability, policy transparency, participatory
  standard-setting, vendor switching costs, and local override mechanisms.
- Compare centralized vendor-controlled AI deployments with
  community-governed, institutionally configurable, or open systems.
- Build governance case studies for churches, Christian schools, charities,
  denominational bodies, interfaith nonprofits, and family-facing tools.
- Produce a benchmark or checklist for whether communities can shape, contest,
  or exit AI systems that affect them.

## Needed Controls

- Avoid assuming local control is always safer than centralized safeguards.
- Include small institutions that lack technical staff.
- Distinguish theological authority, product governance, data governance, and
  operational configuration.
- Account for security, privacy, and abuse-prevention constraints on local
  override.
- Avoid treating subsidiarity as unchecked local power when local leaders or
  institutions may be conflicted, coercive, or abusive.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This complements `FID-020` community co-design and `FID-031` church governance by
measuring structural power, exit rights, audit access, and local agency in the
AI systems that communities procure or inherit.

## Outputs

- Subsidiarity benchmark for faith-facing AI governance.
- Subsidiarity scorecard for faith-facing AI procurement.
- Community-agency checklist for procurement and deployment.
- Case-study framework for centralized vs locally governed AI systems.
- Field brief on power concentration in faith-facing AI.

## Open Questions

- Which AI governance decisions should be local, shared, vendor-controlled, or
  externally regulated?
- What minimum audit and appeal rights should faith communities require?
- How can small institutions gain agency without taking on unrealistic technical
  burdens?

## Ways to Help

- Review subsidiarity indicators and governance scenarios.
- Contribute case studies from churches, schools, nonprofits, and platform deployments.
- Build practical measures for exit rights, audit access, and local override.
- Connect this work to AI governance, civic technology, and ecclesial polity expertise.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can evaluate governance structures for community agency and power
concentration. It cannot guarantee that local control, centralized governance, or
any single institutional model is always the right answer.

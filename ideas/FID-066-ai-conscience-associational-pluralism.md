# FID-066: AI Gatekeepers, Conscience, and Associational Pluralism

Status: `seed`

Track: `broader-faith-safety`

Primary need: technology governance, law and religion, platform research, pluralism and safety review

## Question

Under what technical, contractual, and governance conditions can faith-based
associations and their members use, configure, contest, and exit AI systems in
ways consistent with lawful conscience commitments while respecting the safety,
rights, and dignity of others?

## Why It Matters

AI systems increasingly mediate communication, information access, fundraising,
employment workflows, education, and public participation. When a small number
of platforms or model providers define the available options, they can shape
whether associations can express their commitments, organize around shared
purposes, or obtain a fair hearing when a system's behavior conflicts with their
mission. This question cannot be answered by assuming either unrestricted
customization or centralized control is sufficient.

## Hypothesis

Plural and safe participation is more likely when systems provide intelligible
policies, documented reasons, proportionate appeals, configuration boundaries,
data portability, and feasible exit paths. These conditions must be assessed
alongside protections against harassment, discrimination, coercion, and harmful
use.

## Proposed Method

- Map the technical, contractual, and policy choices that make AI systems
  gateways for association, expression, or organizational work.
- Develop a comparative framework for explanation, appeal, configuration,
  portability, interoperability, and exit.
- Analyze a diverse set of hypothetical and documented use cases involving
  faith-based associations and other worldview communities.
- Convene legal, safety, civil-society, and religious-pluralism reviewers to
  test the framework's tradeoffs and limits.

## Needed Controls

- Treat religious freedom, conscience, equality, safety, and non-coercion as
  potentially competing considerations rather than assuming one overrides all
  others.
- Include multiple faiths and nonreligious worldview communities in comparative
  review.
- Avoid partisan claims, advocacy for legislation, or conclusions about a
  platform without documented evidence and a fair response process.
- Separate private institutional uses from public-facing services and
  employment-related settings.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This extends `FID-013`, `FID-014`, `FID-022`, `FID-040`, and `FID-043`. It
focuses on the institutional and market-gateway conditions for plural
association, not only a model's treatment of religious content or persuasion.

## Outputs

- A taxonomy of AI gatekeeping risks for associations and conscience-sensitive
  workflows.
- A comparative governance framework with case-study and review protocols.
- A public research brief on explanation, appeal, configuration, portability,
  and exit conditions.
- A set of open questions for future empirical and legal research.

## Open Questions

- Which forms of customization protect pluralism, and which enable exclusion or
  harm?
- What constitutes a meaningful appeal when a model's output is probabilistic?
- How can small associations exercise exit rights when they lack technical or
  financial leverage?

## Ways to Help

- Review case scenarios from law and religion, civil society, AI governance, and safety practice.
- Contribute evidence on platform governance, procurement, and portability.
- Identify missing perspectives and pressure-test the framework's tradeoffs.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can clarify and compare institutional conditions for plural,
accountable AI access. It cannot settle contested legal questions, endorse a
political program, or guarantee that a given system will respect conscience in
every context.

# FID-034: AI Supply-Chain Dignity and Faith-Institution Procurement

Status: `seed`

Track: `broader-faith-safety`

Primary need: procurement ethics, labor dignity, environmental responsibility, vendor accountability

## Question

What evidence should faith institutions request about the human labor,
moderation work, data sourcing, compute infrastructure, energy use, minerals,
and downstream exploitation risks behind the AI systems they adopt?

## Why It Matters

Faith institutions may evaluate AI tools by cost, usefulness, safety claims, or
brand trust while missing hidden burdens in the supply chain. Data labeling,
content moderation, scraped data, energy-intensive compute, hardware production,
and extractive mineral supply can all raise dignity and stewardship concerns
before a model ever answers a church, school, or ministry user.

## Hypothesis

General AI procurement checklists will underweight hidden labor, ecological
costs, and exploitative upstream dependencies. Faith institutions need a
practical way to ask vendors for evidence without pretending that small churches
can perform full supply-chain audits on their own.

## Proposed Method

- Map AI supply-chain risk categories relevant to churches, schools, charities,
  publishers, seminaries, and ministries.
- Design a vendor questionnaire covering data labor, content moderation,
  worker protections, dataset provenance, energy reporting, hardware sourcing,
  and auditability.
- Define levels of evidence: public documentation, vendor attestation,
  contract commitments, third-party audit, and unknown or unverifiable claims.
- Identify which claims can be verified publicly, which require vendor
  attestation, and which require third-party audit.
- Produce a lightweight procurement addendum as the first deliverable before
  attempting a full supply-chain assessment framework.

## Needed Controls

- Avoid unverifiable purity tests that no institution can apply.
- Avoid making small faith institutions responsible for solving opaque vendor
  supply chains; the goal is better decision-making and pressure for disclosure.
- Distinguish vendor-level responsibility, cloud-provider responsibility, and
  model-provider responsibility.
- Include smaller vendors and open-source systems without assuming that only
  frontier labs matter.
- Separate environmental reporting, labor dignity, data legitimacy, and safety
  performance.

## Relationship to Existing Ideas

This extends `FID-022` procurement readiness by focusing specifically on labor,
data, compute, environmental, and upstream accountability claims rather than
general institutional readiness.

## Outputs

- AI supply-chain dignity risk map.
- Lightweight procurement addendum for small and mid-sized institutions.
- Vendor questionnaire for faith institutions.
- Evidence rubric for labor, data, compute, and environmental claims.
- Procurement addendum aligned with faith-institution values.

## Open Questions

- What supply-chain disclosures are realistic for small churches and schools to
  request?
- Which indicators best reveal hidden exploitation without overclaiming from
  incomplete evidence?
- How should faith institutions weigh supply-chain risks against clear local
  benefits from an AI tool?

## Ways to Help

- Review labor, content-moderation, environmental, and supply-chain criteria.
- Contribute procurement examples from churches, schools, and nonprofits.
- Identify public data sources for vendor evidence.
- Build a practical scoring rubric for institutional buyers.

## Public Claim Boundary

This work can help institutions ask better supply-chain questions and compare
available evidence. It cannot certify that an AI system is exploitation-free or
environmentally harmless.
